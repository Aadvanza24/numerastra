Moolank, Bhagyank, and Namank Explained

The three core numbers of Vedic numerology. Where Western numerology gives you a life path, Ankashastra gives you a three-layer identity — how you see yourself, where your path leads, and what your name broadcasts into the world.

Published 8 April 2026 · 9 min read · By Numerastra

Vedic numerology, known in Sanskrit as Ankashastra (anka = number, shastra = science), is one of three major numerological traditions alongside Pythagorean and Chaldean. It differs from Western systems in two important ways: it uses three distinct numbers to describe you rather than one dominant "life path," and each number is tied to a specific planet in the Navagraha system.

If you've been reading Western numerology content and it hasn't quite clicked, it's possible the Vedic model will. It's more layered and — for people with Indian or Hindu backgrounds — culturally native.

The three core numbers

1. Moolank — the psychic number

Moolank (mool = root, anka = number) is your "root number," often translated as the psychic number. It's calculated from your birth day only — nothing else. Master numbers are not preserved in Vedic Moolank; everything reduces to 1–9.

Moolank for someone born on the 15th 1 + 5 = 6
Moolank for someone born on the 29th 2 + 9 = 11 → 1 + 1 = 2

The moolank represents how you see yourself — your conscious self-image, the identity you inhabit day to day. It's the first number a Vedic numerologist will ask you about, because it reveals more about your moment-to-moment experience than either of the other two.

2. Bhagyank — the destiny number

Bhagyank (bhagya = destiny, anka = number) is your destiny or fortune number. It's calculated from your full date of birth — day, month, and year — summed and reduced to a single digit.

Bhagyank for 15 March 1992 1 + 5 + 3 + 1 + 9 + 9 + 2 = 30 → 3 + 0 = 3
Bhagyank for 29 November 1985 2 + 9 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 9 + 8 + 5 = 36 → 3 + 6 = 9

If moolank is "who I think I am," bhagyank is "where this soul is heading." It describes your destined path — the broad direction your life will move regardless of the choices you make. Think of moolank as your personality and bhagyank as your trajectory.

Bhagyank fully activates around age 35. Before that, most people are primarily expressing their moolank; after 35, the bhagyank begins to assert itself more strongly. This is why mid-life pivots are so common — you're shifting from one layer of identity to the next.

3. Namank — the name number

Namank (naam = name, anka = number) is your name number, calculated using the Chaldean letter-to-number mapping (never Pythagorean in Vedic tradition). It reveals how your name vibrates into the world — what people feel when they hear it, say it, write it.

Namank for "Priya" using Chaldean values P(8) + R(2) + I(1) + Y(1) + A(1) = 13 → 1 + 3 = 4

Namank is the most changeable of the three. You can't change your birth day or date, but you can adjust the spelling of your name. This is why name correction — a classical Vedic remedy — focuses on bringing your namank into harmony with your moolank and bhagyank.

Why three numbers instead of one?

Western numerology's life path works well as a single theme, but it loses nuance. Vedic numerology keeps the nuance intentionally. Different layers of you show up in different contexts:

  • Moolank shows up in casual encounters, daily decisions, how you show up at work.
  • Bhagyank shows up in big decisions, midlife transitions, the shape of your career over decades.
  • Namank shows up whenever your name is used — business, branding, introductions, signatures, legal documents.

Ideally all three harmonise. When they do, you experience a kind of flow: how you see yourself (moolank), where you're heading (bhagyank), and how the world perceives you (namank) all point the same direction. When they don't harmonise, you get friction — and that friction is the starting point for remedies.

The Navagraha connection

Every Vedic number 1–9 is ruled by one of the nine planets in Hindu astrology, the Navagraha. This is the deepest difference from Western numerology — numbers aren't abstract symbols; they're planetary energies.

Your moolank planet is your lifelong companion. If you're a moolank 3 (Jupiter), you're under Jupiter's influence regardless of what else happens in your chart — it shapes your default worldview. Harmony and friction between the three

Classical Vedic numerology texts give specific friendship, neutrality, and enmity relationships between numbers. The basic idea:

  • Same number across all three (moolank = bhagyank = namank) — extreme amplification. Rare and intense.
  • Friendly combination — smooth flow. Life feels like it's moving with you.
  • Neutral combination — balanced. Life is ordinary, no strong current either way.
  • Enemy combination — friction. Life feels like you're pushing against something. This is where remedies become useful.

When the numbers clash — remedies

If your namank clashes with your moolank or bhagyank, traditional remedies include:

  • Name correction — adjust the spelling of your name (add/remove a letter) to shift your namank into a friendly number. The most common and least invasive.
  • Planetary gemstones — wearing the gemstone of the planet you want to strengthen (e.g., yellow sapphire for Jupiter, pearl for Moon).
  • Mantras — reciting the seed mantra of the relevant planet 108 times daily.
  • Charity on the planet's day — giving to the needy on the day ruled by your clashing planet, as a karmic offering.
  • Colour adjustment — wearing the favourable planet's colours more often.

These are traditional remedies, not miracle cures. Use them as rituals of attention — they work when they focus your intention, which then shapes your choices.

Moolank versus Western life path — why they differ

Your Pythagorean life path and Vedic bhagyank often produce the same number — both are calculated from full DOB. But your moolank (birth day only) will often differ, sometimes significantly.

Example: 29 November 1985. Pythagorean life path = 9. Vedic moolank = 2 (2+9=11→2). These two numbers feel very different — 9 is completion and service, 2 is sensitivity and partnership. Both are accurate; they describe different layers.

This is why our synthesis number matters — it shows where the systems agree and where they diverge, giving you a fuller picture than any single system provides.
